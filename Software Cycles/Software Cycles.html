Software Development Life Cycles

Overview

Software development life cycles (SDLC) help guide teams through the process of building and maintaining software. There are several popular models, each with its own approach, benefits, and challenges. Below are five common models: Cascade (Waterfall), Iterative, Incremental, Spiral, and Prototype. We will compare them based on structure, flexibility, and risk management.

Cascade Model (Waterfall)

The Cascade model is a linear and sequential approach where each phase must be completed before the next one begins. It follows these phases: Requirements, Design, Implementation, Testing, Deployment, and Maintenance.

Pros: Simple to manage, clear milestones.

Cons: Rigid, difficult to adapt to changes, high risk if issues are found late.

Iterative Model

The Iterative model repeats cycles of development in iterations, improving and refining the product at each step. Feedback is incorporated at the end of each iteration.

Pros: Flexibility, easy to adjust requirements during development.

Cons: Can be resource-intensive, especially if many iterations are needed.

Incremental Development Model

This model breaks down the software into smaller, manageable sections (increments), where each part is developed and deployed incrementally. Each new increment builds upon the previous ones.

Pros: Reduces risk, earlier delivery of partial product.

Cons: Complexity can increase over time if not managed well.

Spiral Model

The Spiral model combines iterative development with risk analysis. It consists of multiple phases, where each phase begins with risk assessment and planning, followed by design, implementation, and evaluation.

Pros: Strong focus on risk management, adaptable to changes.

Cons: Can be complex and costly due to the extensive planning and analysis.

Prototype Model

The Prototype model emphasizes building a prototype to understand user requirements before actual development begins. The prototype is then used as a foundation for the final product.

Pros: Helps clarify user needs early on, reduces misunderstandings.

Cons: Can lead to scope creep if not properly managed.

Comparison

Model Structure Flexibility Risk Management
Cascade (Waterfall) Linear, Sequential Low High risk if issues arise late
Iterative Repetitive, Cyclic High Lower risk due to early feedback
Incremental Small Increments Moderate Lower risk through gradual development
Spiral Cyclic with Phases High High focus on risk management
Prototype Prototyping, then Development High Moderate, but can cause scope creep
